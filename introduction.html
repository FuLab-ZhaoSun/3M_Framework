Abstract

Background: Omics-based technologies have significantly advanced precision medicine, relying on high-quality biobanks to meet technical demands. However, universal guidance for end-to-end management of biospecimen collection during patient hospitalization is still lacking, leading to significant bias in subsequent integrated omics analyses.

Aim of Review: This review aims to systematically summarize the requirements of multi-omics technologies for biological samples and propose a standardized multimodal biospecimen management framework.

Key Scientific Concepts: This study proposes a framework encompassing: (1) multimodal biospecimen volume requirements for each omics type; (2) standard protocols for multimodal biospecimen collection, transport, and storage, including tissue, serum, saliva, urine, and faeces; (3) ethical compliance; and (4) quality control (QC) and data management, including metadata collection and other processes. This framework facilitates standardized multimodal biospecimen management, thereby accelerating precision oncology development based on omics technologies.

Introduction Background

Biobanks, encompassing programmatic biomaterials, associated information, and legal considerations involving data security and protection[1, 2] , are cornerstones of biomedical research. Their evolution mirrors a shift from disease-specificity towards multidimensional data integration. The 1990s witnessed the emergence of biobanks, primarily disease-oriented and limited in scope[3-5]. As biological research progressed, complex disease-gene relationships became evident. The Human Genome Project (HGP), aimed at comprehensively exploring human genetic information, concluded successfully in 2003. Subsequently, biobanks primarily supporting genomic research emerged [6-8], yet they faced limitations in data dimensionality[9]. Breakthroughs in high-throughput technologies spurred the systematic integration of omics (e.g., genomics, proteomics, metabolomics), enabling comprehensive or near-comprehensive analysis of cellular components like RNA and DNA[10]. This propelled biobanks towards multi-omics data centres. Notable examples include the UK Biobank, releasing genomic and phenotypic data for 500,000 individuals[11], and The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA), a multi-omics cancer database[12], providing crucial resources for precision medicine.

Impact of Multi-Omics Technologies

The advent of multi-omics technologies has revolutionized biomedical research by enabling systematic exploration of biological systems. These technologies generate large-scale datasets capturing genetic, molecular, and functional dynamics, offering unprecedented insights into disease mechanisms and personalized therapies. However, their success hinges on the availability of high-quality biospecimens, highlighting the critical role of biobanks. Currently, biobanks worldwide operate largely as localized entities, often established to serve specific research objectives, resulting in a fragmented landscape. In this context, constructing multi-omics-oriented biobanks requires integrating multidimensional biomarkers and establishing a standardized framework across platforms to support the entire innovation spectrum, from basic research to clinical translation.

Project Significance and Objectives

This project aims to fill the current gaps in biobank management, particularly regarding the standardization of sample collection and management for multi-omics research. By establishing a comprehensive framework, we expect to address the following key issues:

  • Establish specific requirement standards for different types of biological samples for multi-omics research
  • Standardize the entire process of biological sample collection, processing, and storage
  • Ensure ethical compliance and data security management
  • Provide quality control standards and metadata collection specifications

By achieving these objectives, this project will promote the standardization of multi-omics research and data comparability, accelerating the progress of precision medicine.
